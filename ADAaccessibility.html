Improve ADA Accessibility Systemwide


Updated 22 Aug 2024


Attribution: "MTA Announces First Ever Paratransit Electric Vehicles Joining Access-A-Ride Fleet" by MTAPhotos is licensed under CC BY 2.0 .


The MTA has incomplete ADA accessibility with many train stations lacking features for people with disabilities. Since the 1980s, the MTA has been gradually improving accessibility at key stations, to make 95 percent of all subway and Staten Island Railway stations ADA accessible by 2055. Despite efforts to increase accessibility, only 145 out of 472 New York City Subway stations are currently ADA accessible. This leads to criticism and legal challenges over renovated stations' lack of elevators and ramps. The MTA has faced several lawsuits for not making stations fully accessible, highlighting the ongoing challenges in achieving universal accessibility in the transit system.


In New York City, all buses are ADA accessible, with the entire local bus fleet equipped with semi-low floors and wheelchair ramps, while express buses have high floors and lifts. The implementation of wheelchair accessibility began in 1981, with a significant increase in accessible buses by 1991 following the passing of the ADA. The paratransit service, Access-A-Ride, was introduced to provide transportation for disabled customers who cannot use regular bus or subway services and has faced criticism for being costly, unwieldy, and unreliable. Efforts to improve the service include introducing hands-free intercom systems, expanding the program to accommodate more riders, and purchasing electric vans for the fleet.

As MTA express bus routes only consist of high-floored commuter coaches, the MTA has to start ordering MCI D45 CRT commuter coaches, which offer innovative accessibility features and options for clean diesel or battery-electric configurations, instead of more high-floored commuter coaches. These buses prioritize passenger comfort, safety, and environmental sustainability, providing comprehensive service and support for customers. Electric-powered buses are more efficient, emit fewer emissions, and have lower operating costs than diesel-powered buses with better torque and longevity. They are also quieter and have fewer mechanical components than diesel-powered buses.
